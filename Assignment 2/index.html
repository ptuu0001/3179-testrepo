According to the World Health Organisation (WHO), there are factors known as the determinants of health that influence an individual’s health (World Health Organisation [WHO], 2017). These determinants of health include but are not limited to social, economic situation, physical living environment and individual’s behaviour. This article will explore various factors that can affect individual’s livelihood and see how they compare against other countries.

The World Happiness Report is an annual survey that collates various quality of life factors and bases their ranking on countries on a happiness score (Wikipedia, 2021). The happiness score is determined by individuals answering this question:

‘Please imagine a ladder, with steps numbered from 0 at the bottom to 10 at the top. The top of the ladder represents the best possible life for you and the bottom of the ladder represents the worst possible life for you. On which step of the ladder would you say you personally feel you stand at this time?’ (Helliwell, Huang, Wang, & Norton, 2021).

As the name suggests, a higher score would mean a country/its citizens are happier. While the rest of the graphs and visualisations in this article may not directly contribute to the happiness scoring process, they are definitely still factors that influences an individual’s happiness level. It would seem that most developed countries appear to have relatively happy citizens, while developing countries are more likely to be unhappy.


Though one critique I would like to add on the Happiness Report, is that it doesn’t consider exceptional circumstances that may influence an individual’s psyche. As an example, if a person recently had a break-up, they would likely be at a very low point in their life. If they were to score their happiness, it may not accurately reflect how they feel about their happiness levels overall, since they are still dealing with an emotional event in their life.

Freedom to make life choices is another question that was asked in the World Happiness Report, where people were asked:

‘Are you satisfied or dissatisfied with your freedom to choose what you do with your life?’ (Helliwell, Huang, & Wang, 2021).
Multiple factors can contribute to how this question was answered. Some contexts can include if they are experiencing any economic burden, their current mental wellbeing, their country’s current political landscape or extraneous factors that are out of their control, such as a broken bone limiting their mobility; again a weakness of the happiness report.


For perception of corruption, users were asked if:

‘Corruption was widespread throughout the government or not’ and ‘Is corruption widespread within businesses or not?’ (Helliwell et al., 2021).
Having a lower score, would mean the country would be perceived as more corrupt.
It mostly does seem that having corrupt governments would correlate to a lower happiness score, but it does seem to affect in the scoring of ‘freedom to make life choices’. Perhaps this may indicate that despite being aware of their corrupt governments, it does not impede their daily activities, so they still feel a sense of freedom. What is your interpretation?


One more thing I like to point out is an outlier for the country Rwanda (you can see it by adjusting the happiness score range to be between 2.5 and 3.5, located on the top-right corner). Despite having a low corruption score (0.41) and high ‘freedom to make life choices’ score (0.56), it seems to be quite an unhappy country with a score of 3.33.

In the context of the Happiness Report, social support refers to the question:

‘If you were in trouble, do you have relatives or friends you can count on to help you whenever you need them, or not?’ (Helliwell et al., 2021).
It is not difficult to see how the correlation of social support affects happiness. Forming close and meaningful relationships and having a supportive network of friends, is a contributing factor in an individual’s social health. It should be noted that the question does not refer to external services such as therapy or consultations.


Healthy life expectancy, as the name suggests, is how well an individual is expected to live a fulfilling life, without worrying about illness or injuries that can affect their daily basis of life. We can see how having social support from others correlates to people being more likely happy, while still being able to lead healthy lives.

We can see that overtime life expectancy has risen for the entire world, including countries on an individual level. Factors such as advancements in medicines, better understanding in managing and treating diseases, better safety standards, introduction of hygienic practices and access to medical care all contributed to higher overall life expectancy (Friedman, 2015).


Notice how around 1914 and 1945 there was a considerable dip in the average life expectancy for a few countries, especially for Russia. This was because of World War I and World War II, where millions lost their lives fighting in the global conflict causing a dramatic drop in life expectancy for participating countries. However, there is still a disproportionate difference between developing and developed countries (not represented that well in the line graph, due to the small dataset). India, still being a developing country is overtaken by most developed countries by at least 10 years. This is often generalised to the lack of access to standard health care services.


Being able to access healthcare is one of the most important factors on affecting the average life expectancy in any country. It is in a country’s best interest to provide universal healthcare system, that is accessible for everyone, regardless of economic and environmental circumstances (Shimizu, n.d.). The treatment or prevention of illness and injuries all fall under the umbrella term of healthcare. Having healthcare systems with a strong foundation, means an individual can recover quickly and resume to enjoying their life without being burdened by any unexpected caveats, such as high payment fees.


Countries such as South Korea, Denmark and Australia are widely regarded as countries with the best healthcare systems in the world (World Population Review, 2021). They all share in common having a free universal healthcare, that is accessible to all their citizens regardless of their circumstances.

The economic freedom index is an annual ranking created by The Heritage Foundation, that measures the economic freedom from around the world. As stated by foundation, a country is considered to be economically free when ‘individuals are free to work, produce, consume, and invest in any way they please’ and ‘governments allow labor, capital, and goods to move freely, and refrain from coercion or constraint of liberty…’ (The Heritage Foundation, 2021a).


There are twelve factors which are used to determine the economic freedom score. They are grouped as:

  • Rule of law
  • Government size
  • Regulatory efficiency
  • Open markets
However, for the purpose of keeping this article at a reasonable length, we are only going to focus on the category regulatory efficiency and one aspect from the open market category, to give the data a nice mix.


Labour freedom (regulatory efficiency):
Labour freedom is a quantitative measurement where various factors concerning a country’s labour market and regulations, such as minimum wage or mandatory severance pay are considered to the score (The Heritage Foundation, 2021b).

Business freedom (regulatory efficiency):
Business freedom measure the efficacy of government regulation of businesses (The Heritage Foundation, 2021c). Such factors that are measured include starting business costs or starting business minimum capital.

Monetary freedom (regulatory efficiency):
Monetary freedom measures how stable the market is, by measuring the price stability and price controls (The Heritage Foundation, 2021d). Ideally, intervention and inflation should be kept at a minimum to not disrupt market activity.

Trade freedom (open markets):
Trade freedom is a composite measurement of both the absence of tariffs and non-tariff barriers that impede the imports and exports of goods and services (The Heritage Foundation, 2021e). Countries ideally should be able to internationally trade with each other, without barriers that impede the flow of goods and services to ensure a free market.
